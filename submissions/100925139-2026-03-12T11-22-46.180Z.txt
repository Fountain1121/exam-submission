crurifragium (crura fracture) was a Roman practice used during crucifixion to hasten the death of a victim. The exemption of Jesus from this procedure fulfilled Old Testament prophecy and serves as a significant proof of His identity as the promised Messiah.

-Crurifragium refers to the breaking of the legs of a crucified victim in order to speed up death. During crucifixion, victims often died slowly from suffocation because they had to push up on their legs to breathe. When soldiers broke the legs, the victim could no longer lift the body to breathe and death occurred quickly.
This practice was sometimes carried out when authorities needed the victims to die quickly, especially before important events such as the Jewish Sabbath.
In Jewish custom, bodies were not supposed to remain on the cross during the Sabbath. Therefore, Roman soldiers sometimes broke the legs of crucified victims so they would die before the Sabbath began.

When the soldiers came to the three men on the crosses:
They broke the legs of the two criminals crucified with Jesus.
When they came to Jesus, they found that He was already dead, so they did not break His legs.
Instead, one soldier pierced His side with a spear, and blood and water flowed out.
In John 19:32–34
-The Old Testament foretold that the Messiah’s bones would not be broken.
Prophecies:
Psalm 34:20 – “He guards all his bones; not one of them is broken.”
Exodus 12:46 – Concerning the Passover lamb: “Nor shall you break one of its bones.”
These scriptures pointed prophetically to the coming Messiah, who would be the true Passover Lamb.

-The exemption of Jesus from crurifragium demonstrates that:
Jesus is the fulfillment of Old Testament prophecy.
He is the perfect Passover Lamb, whose sacrifice brings salvation.
His death occurred under divine plan, not merely human circumstances
